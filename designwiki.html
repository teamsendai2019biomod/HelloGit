Design


To realize the hydrogel that exhibits swelling and shrinking like muscles in response to a DNA signal, the polymer constituting the gel is crosslinked with an actuator DNA complex that exhibits extending and regression. Regarding swelling, some effective way to swell hydrogel while maintaining the crosslinking points has already been reported [2]. These reactions are indeed efficient and effective, but it lacks shrinking ability that enable reversible and repeatable movement. Therefore, we decided to design a novel HCR called RHCR (Reversible HCR) that can cause extending (fig. 3) and regressing (fig. 4). Before going to the exposition of RHCR, we will explain about HCR below, which is the based idea of RHCR.(fig. 1).

Principle of HCR maintaining the cross-linking point

fig. 1 Principle of HCR maintaining the cross-linking point


DNA A and DNA R are the cross-linking points of the ge in the initial state. In a HCR that maintains the cross-linking point, hairpin H1 binds to a single-stranded toe-hold section of A and R, and then a four-way branch migration follows (state 2-3). As a result, R switches from A to H1 (state 4). H2 binds to the newly exposed toe-hold end of H1, and a four-way branch migration occurs again (state 6-7). This replaces R from H1 to H2 (state 8). In the result of this reaction, H1 hybridize to the newly exposed sticky ends a* and x* again, which leads to a four-way branch migration.
By this cycle, the DNA complex extends while maintaining the crosslinking point. Because there is no toehold left on the A side, regression can not be realized by HCR. In order to carry out regression by HCR, we inserted d, e, f, g, and h domains into our designed DNA.The reaction flow is below.

fig.2 RHCR extending reaction

＜RHCR extending reaction＞
1. H1 approaches crosslinking point
2. H1 binds to sticky ends of A and R
3. A four-way branch migration occurs
4. The R hybridization bonds are switched from A to H1
5. H2 approaches crosslinking point
6. H2 binds to Sticky Ends of A and R
7. A four-way branch migration occurs
8. The R hybridization bonds are switched from H1 to H2
9. H1 approaches crosslinking point
10. H1 domain a and domain x* bind with domain a* of H2 and the domain x of R
11. A four-way branch migration occurs
12. The R hybridization bonds are switched from H2 to H1
13. H2 joins domain c* and domain y with domain c of H1 and the domain y* of R as the toehold
14. A four-way branch migration occurs
15. The R hybridization bonds are switched from H1 to H2

In the result of this reaction, the toeholds of oligonucleotide on step 15 has the same structure as slide 1. With this cycle, the HCR extends toward right while maintaining the cross- linking point. In contrast to conventional HCR, in our designed DNA we have toeholds for another hairpin DNAs called EH1 (H1 eraser), which regress HCR by being added. The regressive reaction flow is shown below.

fig. 3 RHCR regressive reaction

＜RHCR regressive reaction＞
1. EH1 approaches crosslinking point
2. EH1 toehold hybridizes with the e domain of H1 and the d domain of A
3. A four-way branch migration occurs
4. The hybridization continues further by the opening ring of c* in EH1
5. EH1-H1 releaser approaches crosslinking point
6. EH1-H1 releaser bind with the toehold in EH1
7. EH1 and A are untied
8. EH1 and H1 are released from crosslink
9. The dangling end of A hybridizes with H1
10. A hybridizes with H1 untied bond of H1 and H2
11. H2 is released from crosslink
12. Another EH1 approaches crosslinking point

In the result of this reaction, the toeholds of oligonucleotide on step 21 has the same structure as slide 1. The difference is that the sequence has been shortened with H1 and H2 being released from the sequence. Then, this reaction repeats until the last H2 in the sequence because the f* domain in the H2 is needed to release the other H2 as shown in slide 19.

If H1 and H2 are present in this state, the extending reaction starts again on the R side, and RHCR shows a dynamic behavior that extends and regresses in response to DNA signals. If H1 and H2 do not exist, RHCR is maintained in a regressive state. The final state shown in fig. 5 is at least 8nm shorter than the original state of several H1 and H2 in series, so we can insist that RHCR has a regressive function.
