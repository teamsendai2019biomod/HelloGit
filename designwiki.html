Design


To realize gel that exhibits swelling and shrinking like muscles in response to a DNA signal, the polymer constituting the gel is crosslinked with an actuator DNA that exhibits elongation and regression. Regarding swelling, some effective way to swell hydro gel while maintaining the crosslinking points has already been reported [2]. This swelling reaction is indeed efficient and effective, but it lacks shrinking reactions that enable reversible and lasting movement. Therefore, we considered a novel HCR called RHCR (Reversible HCR) that can cause extending (fig.3) and regressing (fig.4) as an actuator for a gel that exhibits swelling and shrinking.

Principle of HCR maintaining the cross-linking point

fig. 1 Principle of HCR maintaining the cross-linking point


DNA A and DNA R are the cross-linking points of the gel. In a special HCR that maintains the cross-linking point, hairpin H1 binds to the sticky ends of A and R, and a four-way branch migration occurs (state 2~3). As a result, R switches from A to H1 (state 4). H2 binds to the newly exposed sticky end of H1, and a four-way branch migration occurs (state 6~7). This replaces R from H1 to H2 (state 8). By this cycle, the DNA complex extends in the R direction while maintaining the crosslinking point.

In RHCR, d, e, f, g, and h domains are required for regression in addition to the domains a, b, c, x, and y required for conventional HCR elongation.

＜RHCR elongation reaction＞
1. H1 approaches crosslinking point
2. H1 binds to sticky ends of A and R
3. A four-way branch migration occurs
4. The R hybridization bonds are switched from A to H1
5. H2 approaches crosslinking point
6. H2 binds to Sticky Ends of A and R
7. A four-way branch migration occurs
8. The R hybridization bonds are switched from H1 to
9. H1 approaches crosslinking point
10. H1 domain a and domain x* bind with domain a* of H2 and the domain x of R
11. A four-way branch migration occurs
12. The R hybridization bonds are switched from H2 to H1
13. H2 joins domain c* and domain y with domain c of H1 and the domain y* of R as the toehold
14. A four-way branch migration occurs
15. The R hybridization bonds are switched from H1 to H2

With this cycle, the film extends in the R direction while maintaining the crosslinking point.

fig.3 RHCR regression reaction

＜RHCR regressive reaction＞
1. EH1 approaches crosslinking point
2. EH1 toehold hybridizes with the e domain of H1 and the d domain of A
3. A four-way branch migration occurs
4. The hybridization continues further by the opening ring of c* in EH1
5. EH1-H1 releaser approaches crosslinking point
6. EH1-H1 releaser bind with the toehold in EH1
7. EH1 and A are untied
8. EH1 and H1 are released from crosslink
9. The dangling end of A hybridizes with H1
10. A hybridizes with H1 untied bond of H1 and H2
11. H2 is released from crosslink
12. Another EH1 approaches crosslinking point


In the end, the sequence of oligonucleotide on slide 21 has the same structure as slide 1. The difference is that the sequence has been shortened with H1 and H2 being released from the sequence. Then, this reaction repeats until the last H2 in the sequence because the f* domain in the H2 is needed to release the other H2 as shown in slide 19.

If H1 and H2 are present in this state, the elongation reaction starts again on the R side, and RHCR shows a dynamic behavior that repeat swelling and shrinking. If H1 and H2 do not exist, RHCR is maintained in a regressive state. The final stage shown in fig. 5 is much shorter than the original state of several H1 and H2 in series, so there is no doubt that RHCR has a regressive function.
