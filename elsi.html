“ELSI” is an abbreviation for “ethical, legal and social implications”.Science and technology are key drivers of development. Technological andscientific revolutions underpin economical advances, as well as improvementsin health systems, education, and infrastructure. However, in some cases, theymay have an unexpected impact on society. The area of ELSI research is toforesee such effects and consider how to deal with them.

The term ELSI appeared in the “Human Genome Project” that started in theUnited States in 1990. The Human Genome Project is a research project thatattempts to decipher all human genetic information (human genome). Theimpact that human genome research can have is not limited to doctors andpatients, but it affects everyone, extending to society as a whole. For thisreason, ELSI research needs to be conducted from various perspectives,including the general public, policymakers, and companies. ELSI research thushas an interdisciplinary and cross-disciplinary character, and therefore can beapplied not only to human genome research, but also to today's various cutting-edge research and technologies.

In modern society, technology is overflowing, and new technology is bornalmost every minute. In addition to the fact that division of labor andspecialization has proceeded, it is almost impossible for the ordinary citizen tograsp and fully understand every technology used in their daily lives. However,trust from society is indispensable in order for new research to progress andtechnology to advance and become implemented in daily life. This is why it isimportant for researchers whom have expertized knowledge and are most likelyto be the founding fathers of such new technologies, to consider ELSI. Theymust contribute to the safety and welfare of the citizens, taking the initiative ingaining their trust. Therefore, here we will discuss and describe our project fromall ethical, legal and social viewpoints, and deepen the understanding of ourproject from a different angle.

Ethical Issues

The ethical issues in this project are mainly those involving DNA handling andvector handling during artificial DNA synthesis. In general, in experiments involving DNA, in fields such as genetic recombination, the safety associatedtherewith is a problem. However, in this study there is no need for this to beconsidered, since DNA is used as a material rather than genetic information.Strict management is required for the handling of ordered DNA, and it is anobligation to inactivate it at the time of disposal; we confirm these rules wereabided in this study.
In addition, the DNA we handled in our experimented were ordered andsynthesized by an outside company (Eurofins Genomics) where phage DNA wasused for building the scaffolds or DNA origami. Phage is a microscopic structurethat may self-replicate using the cells of other organisms. The following is true:“phage has its own genes, and can grow by using the cells of other organisms”;however, since it does not have an actual cell of its own, which is the smallestunit of an organism, phage is not defined as a living organism, according to thedefinition of life. This is why normal provisions regarding experiments involvingliving organisms cannot be directly applied.
For instance, although the current animal experiment provisions in Japan has“Fundamental Guidelines for Proper Conduct of Animal Experiment and RelatedActivities inAcademic Research Institutions”(2006), which is based on the “InternationalGuiding Principles for Biomedical Research” published by the Council forInternational Organizations of Medical Sciences (CIOMS) (1985, revised 2012),regulations concerning phage are not dealt with.
Regardless, as mentioned in our FUTURE section, this research is aimed at thedevelopment of biomaterials with biocompatibility in the future. Thus along thecourse of development towards application, ethical guidelines concerninghuman and animal application such as the following: “Ethical Guidelines forMedical and Health Research Involving Human Subjects” (2014) and“Basic Guidelines for Proper Conduct of Animal Testing and Related Activities in theResearch Institutions under the Jurisdiction of the MHLW” (2006, revised 2015),must be considered seriously.

Legal Issues

Upon the development of synthetic biology, genetic engineering in particular,various international outlines have been defined, including the “Convention onBiological Diversity” (1992, Rio Earth Summit), “Act on the conservation andsustainable use of biological diversity through regulation on the use of genetically modified organisms” (2003) (commonly known as “Cartagena Act”),etc. Consequently, legislation in Japan is progressing, and the handling of DNAas genetic information is strictly regulated. However, the handling of DNA andRNA molecules as materials used in molecular robotics is not defined in Japannor in other countries, and the management is entrusted to the person incharge of the experiment.
However, many of the designs proposed in molecular robotics are to be appliedto human bodies. It is necessary to develop laws based on the possibility thatthese designed will be implemented in society. Since there are almost noproceeding examples of technology using biomolecules applied to the humanbody, when considering clinical trials of implementing drug delivery forexample, it is necessary to carefully carry out clinical trials based on ethicalguidelines on clinical practice in addition to complying with the “MinisterialOrdinance on Good Clinical Practice for Drugs” (or GCP, short for Good ClinicalPractice)(2012).

Social Issues

Molecular robotics is one of the prominent fields where future development isexpected from the possibility of social implementation of the research. However, as of now it is still much under-recognized, and perhaps it could besaid that most people are completely unfamiliar with the field. In truth, evensynthetic biology, which lies as the basic foundation of molecular robotics stillhas a relatively low recognition rate; this can be told from the survey performedin 2010 in Japan, involving over 1500 university students and general citizens,where only approximately 15% of the respondents replied to have heard of theword "synthetic biology". Thus it can be predicted that it will still takeconsiderable time for the designs proposed in molecular robotics to be actuallyapplied in real life.
Moreover, although DNA is only used as a material in molecular robotics, ingeneral, DNA has a strong image as “genetic information” and the field ofmolecular robotics is mistakenly recognized due to concerns about technologysuch as genetic engineering. Perhaps due to compulsory education in Japanmainly teaching DNA only dealt as genetic information, DNA is generallyrecognized as equivalent to gene. Furthermore, according to the consumerintention survey regarding GMOs by the Consumer Affairs Agency, the percentage of respondents who answered to have anxiety about geneticallymodified foods reached 47.7%, while even out of those responding to nothaving anxieties, more than 80% answered to evade genetically modified foods.In light of this, it seems that the social perception of DNA needs to change inorder for molecular robotics technology such as this research that usesartificially synthesized DNA as a material to be implemented in society. Inaddition, a challenge in improving social awareness will be to ensure that theinvented design and technology are safe.
