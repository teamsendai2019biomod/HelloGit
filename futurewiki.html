AAAA

The controllable expansion and shrinking of polyacrymide gel make the gel is like moving in directional movement. The application of controllable directional movement of hydrogel can be utilized as framework of tiny robot. Tiny robots at the micro- and nanoscale have recently attracted great attention because of their unprecedented features and functions as well as enormous potential applications, especially in the areas of health care and biomedicine (1) Tiny robots, as like normal robot, needs to be controllable and programable through given signal. There are various signals that’s usually used for tiny robots, such as DNAstrands, heat, light, and electric potential. For example, Murata et al. (2017) has published a paper about liposome that changes shape in response to DNA strands. However, the movement of liposome is not directional and reversible. In our case, the gel movement is controllable and reversible. Thus, the gel can be developed as a drug carrier in the future. An indispensable property of hydrogel for drug carrier is the biocompatibility of hydrogels, which could be defined as the ability of a material to be in contact with the body organs without any damages for the surrounding tissues and without triggering any undesirable response. Our gel function is not limited just for drug carrier and tiny robots. Hydrogel has unique properties of such as high-water content, softness, flexibility and biocompatibility (2). These characteristics resembles living tissue opens up application for tissue engineering and wound dressings. Tissue engineering is a more recent application of hydrogels, in which they can be applied as space filling agents, as delivery vehicles for bioactive substances or as three- dimensional structures that organize cells and present stimuli to ensure the development of a required tissue (2). The bioactive substances are substituted to the gel, and then the gel is controlled from outside body to move to specific tissue for healing broken tissue inside the body. In material perspectives, doping can be added to the hydrogel to change its conductivity property. Due to the mechanical similarity to biointerfaces such as human skin, conductive hydrogels have been primarily utilized as bioelectrodes, specifically neuroprosthetic electrodes, in an attempt to replace metallic electrodes by enhancing the mechanical properties and long-term stability of the electrodes within living organisms (3). The swelling and shrinking capabilities of the gel can be used to insert the gel to narrow and unreachable place inside the body. We can control the coverage by shrinking/swelling the gel from outside of body.
