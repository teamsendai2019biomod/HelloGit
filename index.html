Gel muscle

~powered by DNA~

Background



The existing robotics has developed in the direction of making accurate and predictable systems consisted of rigid bodies. However, nature frequently performs more than the rigid robotics can actually do, and recently there has been the trend of soft robotics which aims to imitate the natural world [1].
Most notably as a material for soft robots, hydrogel has an excellent value in terms of its biological compatibility and environmental response. Various possible means such as pH control or thermal modulation are known as ways of size regulation of such gel [2]. Among those means, Hybridization Chain Reaction (HCR), an elongation reaction of linear DNA, is reported that it can swell hydro gel hundredfold the volume [3]. The HCR used in the study is specific in that linear DNA extends without losing the connectivity between the crosslinking points throughout the whole reaction. The gel can swell by elongating the crosslink between the matrix polymers. This dynamic change of hydro gel is expected to be applied to metamorphic robots or actuators such as muscles of soft robots.

Problem



It is necessary to regulate reversible swelling and shrinking reactions to apply dynamic size change of hydrogel which is occurred by HCR to actuators such as muscles (fig. 1).
To realize such a reversible regulation, it is needed to proceed the reactions of elongation and regression in response to external signals without losing the connectivity between the crosslinking points. Here, we decided to design a swelling and shrinking system of hydrogel which linear DNA can keep its crosslinking points with polymers by adding signal DNA from outside. This method takes the advantage of interface between hydrogel and external ambient.
